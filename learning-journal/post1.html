Form Design Best Practices

I can think of 5 main principles behind form design best practices according to Medium's “Best practices for form design” article: accessibility, interaction costs, priming, usability, and user-friendliness. These seem pretty universal in web and interactive media design and always directly apply to the projects I work on in this field. In my practice, I either ensure intuitive, flowing, and readable design or point out faults in these areas along with a lack of contrast when I'm also trying to come up with unique, eye-catching illustrations. In upcoming entries for my portfolio of work - which will include form design in the current class I'm in - the 20 points outlined in the Medium article will prove useful in balancing between a checklist covering the 5 overarching best practice principles I mentioned and experimenting with unique and ambitious design ideas.

Online Form Example

An online form I've used before are surveys. An example that demonstrated best practices was a 5-7 minute survey to create a Game Motivation Profile on Quantic Foundry's gaming research website. All questions were around 6 or fewer items, so the fields where you click or type input were either a form of horizontal radio buttons or a vertical list of text entry fields. The interface was simple and readable, including indications and prompts to help figure out what to do. Other features were a percentage progress bar to help indicate how far you were in the longer-form survey and auto-fill popups as you're typing in your entry. It's pretty neat and flowing in usability.
